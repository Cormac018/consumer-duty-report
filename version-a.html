Executive Summary

1.1 Overall Assessment

This is the first combined Consumer Duty Annual Board Report covering both Uinsure Ltd and Uinsure Commercial Ltd. In prior years, separate reports were produced for each entity. The combined format reflects the shared Board, shared governance infrastructure, and complementary operating models of the two businesses, enabling the Board to assess consumer outcomes holistically across the group.

Satisfactory with improvements required.

Uinsure's full-year complaint upheld rate was 44.1% across all four complaint registers — below the 55% FCA threshold. Price optimisation — live and delivering

WTW price optimisation went live in November 2025 and reached 100% of new business from January 2026, without adverse impact on customer price — retail premiums continued to decline. The UIA household fee differential (£48 vs £36) was also resolved through the optimisation rollout.

Governance maturation — Risk Committee and Customer Forum

The Risk Committee (chaired by Non-Executive Director (NED) Jeremy Marsden, established Q3 2025) has met four times and has substantively improved the firm's oversight of conduct and consumer risks. Open actions across the business reduced from 172 at end-2025 to 25 by June 2026. The Customer Forum (chaired by Andy Murphy, monthly) now provides a structured forum for tracking all live customer issues, reviewing a 44-metric Customer Lifecycle Dashboard, and driving company-wide accountability.

Governance and Accountability Framework

2.1 Governance Structure

This section explains the internal committees and meetings through which the Board and senior management oversee how customers are treated. Consumer Duty oversight at Uinsure operates through three interlocking governance forums, each with distinct roles and cadences, providing multi-layered scrutiny of customer outcomes:

Risk Committee

Quarterly

Established Q3 2025 in response to the commitment made in the 2025 Board Report. The Risk Committee is chaired by Jeremy Marsden (Non-Executive Director) and takes its authority directly from the Board. It meets quarterly and its membership includes the full Executive Committee alongside the NED Chair. It reviews the Consumer Duty Annual Report structure, considers customer vital signs data, oversees compliance monitoring, and escalates material risks to the Board. Since launch (Q3 2025), open actions across the business have been reduced from 172 at end of 2025 to 25 by June 2026 — a direct indicator of the Committee's effectiveness in driving accountability. In June 2026, Jeremy Marsden requested to attend the Customer Forum to further deepen NED engagement with customer outcomes data.

Customer Forum

Monthly

Established during the reporting period as a formalised monthly governance forum chaired by Andy Murphy (Head of Customer Experience). The forum has broad cross-functional attendance (22 members) and reviews two standing items at every meeting: live customer issues and remediation activity (tracked against a risk-rated incidents log), and the Customer Lifecycle Dashboard (44 metrics, monthly, sourced from Davies, Covéa, Ageas, Integra, URIS and iCasework). The Forum has formal governance support and produces a documented actions log reviewed at every session.

Product and Pricing Committee

Oversees all product reviews, pricing decisions, and fair value assessments. Reviews pricing activity and provides assurance that price remedy rules are adhered to. Approves all fair value assessments and reviews ancillary product performance data.

Consumer Duty — standing agenda item
Risk Committee (quarterly), Customer Forum (monthly), Product and Pricing Committee, ExCo (via objectives and KPIs).
Escalation to Board
Consumer Duty material risks and the annual report are escalated directly to the Board via the Risk Committee. The NED Chair provides the linkage between operational governance and Board-level scrutiny.

2.2 Consumer Duty Champion

The FCA removed the requirement for a dedicated Consumer Duty Champion in 2025. The business considered this position carefully and concluded that, given all Uinsure Senior Managers hold Consumer Duty accountability through their individual SM&CR Statements of Responsibility, a separate figurehead role was not proportionate and risked implying that Consumer Duty is the responsibility of one individual rather than embedded across the organisation. Consumer Duty is instead embedded through ExCo objectives and KPIs, and through the governance forums described in Section 2.1.

4.1 Target Market Definitions

Uinsure Ltd — core target market

Uinsure's core target market has traditionally been the UK retail consumer accessing insurance through an advised channel — typically an Independent Financial Adviser (IFA) or mortgage adviser at the point of taking out or refinancing a mortgage. These customers value quality of cover and have been guided by a professional through the insurance decision.

This has been evolving since early 2025. A series of migration campaigns brought in a broader customer cohort with different characteristics — including older customers, those more accustomed to telephony than digital, and those from legacy systems with different service expectations. This evolution is accelerating further with the onboarding of new bancassurance and retail partners (NatWest, M&S, LBG, Barclays), which will bring customers who may never have engaged with an independent adviser and who expect a more direct, digital-first experience.

The firm actively monitors its target market definitions and updates them as the customer base evolves. The 2026–2028 strategy explicitly reflects this broadening, and the fair value framework and product governance processes are reviewed accordingly.

Uinsure Commercial Ltd — target market

UCL is the specialist part of the business, serving customers and properties that don't fit a standard home insurance policy.

UCL's target market is the UK retail consumer or landlord with a specialist or non-standard insurance need — properties with previous claims (that fall outside of Uinsure's standard acceptable criteria), high flood or subsidence risk, unoccupied properties, properties undergoing works, mid-to-high net worth, portfolio landlords, Houses in Multiple Occupation (HMOs), and holiday lets. The majority of UCL's customers are retail consumers and are treated as such. Gross Written Premium (GWP, the total value of policies sold) has grown from £8.8m to £12.4m during the reporting period (+41%). Approximately 62% of the book is household (non-standard), 33% property owners, and the remainder ancillary products. SME packages have been closed to new business by Product and Pricing Committee (P&PC) decision to simplify the offering and reduce the risk of errors and omissions.

UCL's non-standard book profile carries a higher inherent potential for customers with characteristics of vulnerability and a greater E&O risk than a standard home insurance book — both of which require tighter operational management as the business scales. Saga has selected Uinsure Specialist as its main partner for all non-standard home business, further validating the proposition.

UCL accepts business from its broker network of approximately 18,000 brokers, with Uinsure referrals now representing the largest single source of leads.

Davies requires careful interpretation. Its reported YTD acceptance rate for 2026 is 60%, below the 71.9% target. However, a formal root cause review completed by Davies in June 2026 has definitively confirmed that this variance is a definitional and methodology issue, not a genuine difference in customer outcomes. Davies' operating model registers all customer contacts as formal claims — including enquiries where the customer ultimately does not proceed — in order to validate coverage and capture claim intent. Other panel insurers filter these interactions out at first notification, meaning their acceptance rates are not calculated on a comparable basis.

Davies' review confirms that decision quality is accurate and consistent, with no evidence of systemic error or inappropriate declinature. A five-month retrospective analysis identified 103 cases that could have been reclassified as enquiries under a revised enquiry-led framework — delivering a conservative minimum uplift of +8 percentage points (60% → 68%), with further improvement expected in a live environment. May 2026 already achieved 77% under the current model, above the 71.9% target.

Davies has proposed an enquiry-led framework that introduces clearer criteria for identifying and categorising enquiries at first notification, aligning methodology with prevailing panel practices. A tripartite discussion between Davies, Uinsure, and Allianz to agree implementation is the recommended next step. Allianz is additionally moving claims handling in-house by end 2026.

Customer sentiment data — measured through Net Promoter Score (NPS), a measure of how likely customers are to recommend Uinsure — shows Claims NPS consistently above the 40 target, consistent with the finding that decision quality has not been an issue — customers are not experiencing worse outcomes, they are experiencing a different registration methodology.

This is a material regulatory development published on the same day this report is being finalised. Consultation closes 18 September 2026; the FCA expects to publish a policy statement and any new rules in Q1 2027.

The FCA is consulting on four specific proposals:

  1. Remove business with non-UK customers from the Duty's scope
  2. Make it clearer where the Duty applies and where it does not
  3. Clarify when and how firms can rely on each other in distribution chains, and how to apply the Duty more proportionately
  4. Explain the interaction between the Duty and other product governance rules

The FCA has acknowledged that "in some areas the Duty is being applied more widely, and more intensively, than intended — particularly in wholesale markets and complex distribution chains." Appendix I — Glossary of Terms and Abbreviations

This glossary is provided so that all readers of this report, regardless of their familiarity with insurance or regulatory terminology, can engage fully with its content.

Term Meaning
ADAccidental Damage — an optional insurance cover for damage caused by accident
APRAnnual Percentage Rate — the cost of borrowing expressed as a yearly rate, used here for premium finance (paying for insurance in instalments)
BAUBusiness as Usual — work that continues as part of normal day-to-day operations, rather than as a one-off project
BSBuilding Society
BTLBuy-to-Let — insurance for landlords renting out a property
CFCCredit Finance Charge — the additional cost added to a premium when a customer chooses to pay in monthly instalments rather than in full
CPDContinuing Professional Development — ongoing training to maintain and develop professional skills
CSATCustomer Satisfaction Score — a customer-reported rating of their satisfaction with a service or interaction
DDDirect Debit
DPIAData Protection Impact Assessment — a formal review of how a new process or technology affects the privacy of personal data
E&OErrors and Omissions — a category of risk relating to mistakes made in giving advice or arranging insurance
EDIElectronic Data Interchange — automated, computer-to-computer exchange of data between Uinsure and an insurer
ExCoExecutive Committee — the senior leadership team of the business
FCAFinancial Conduct Authority — the UK regulator responsible for the conduct of financial services firms, including Uinsure
FV / FVAFair Value / Fair Value Assessment — a formal check that a product gives customers value for money relative to its cost and benefits
GIGeneral Insurance — the category of insurance covering risks such as home, motor, and similar (as opposed to life insurance or investments)
GPGross Profit
GWPGross Written Premium — the total value of insurance premiums sold, before deducting costs
HMOHouse in Multiple Occupation — a rental property shared by multiple unrelated tenants
IFAIndependent Financial Adviser
ISMSInformation Security Management System — the framework of policies and controls that protect data and information security
ISOIn this report, Information Security Officer — the role responsible for data and information security (not to be confused with the International Organization for Standardization)
KRIKey Risk Indicator — a measurable indicator used to track and flag emerging risks before they become problems
MIManagement Information — data and reporting used by the business to monitor performance
MTAMid-Term Adjustment — a change made to a policy during its term, such as updating an address
NBNew Business — a newly sold policy, as distinct from a renewal
NEDNon-Executive Director — a Board member who is not part of the day-to-day executive management team, providing independent oversight
NPSNet Promoter Score — a measure of how likely customers are to recommend Uinsure to others
P&PCProduct and Pricing Committee — the internal committee responsible for overseeing product design, pricing, and fair value
PFPremium Finance — the option for customers to pay for their insurance in monthly instalments rather than a single annual payment
PIFPolicies In Force — the number of live, active policies at a given point in time
PRINPrinciples for Businesses — the FCA's overarching rulebook setting out the standards firms must meet, within which the Consumer Duty sits
QAQuality Assurance — internal checks on the quality and accuracy of customer interactions, such as listening back to sales calls
RAGRed/Amber/Green — a simple traffic-light rating system used throughout this report to indicate performance against a target
RCARoot Cause Analysis — a structured method for identifying the underlying cause of a problem, rather than just its symptoms
RFIRequest for Information — an initial enquiry sent to potential suppliers to gather information ahead of a formal tender
RFPRequest for Proposal — a formal invitation to suppliers to submit a detailed proposal, typically following an RFI
SLAService Level Agreement — an agreed standard of service, such as how quickly calls should be answered
SMESmall and Medium-sized Enterprise — a small business customer, as distinct from a retail consumer
TPAThird Party Administrator — an external company that manages claims or policy administration on behalf of an insurer
UATUser Acceptance Testing — testing carried out before a new system or feature goes live, to confirm it works as intended
UIAA historical customer fee category referenced in prior reporting; the underlying definition is being confirmed and will be clarified in the next version of this report
VCVulnerable Customer(s) — customers with characteristics of vulnerability, as defined under the FCA's Consumer Duty
